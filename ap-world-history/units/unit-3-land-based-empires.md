# Unit 3: Land-Based Empires (1450-1750)

## Overview
This unit examines how rulers used various methods to build and maintain power in land-based empires. The rise of gunpowder technology transformed warfare and state-building. Key focus: How did empires expand and maintain control?

---

## Key Concepts

### 3.1 Empires Expand
- **Gunpowder technology** revolutionized warfare
- Professional armies replaced feudal levies
- Empires expanded through military conquest
- Centralized bureaucracies administered territories

### 3.2 Empires Administer
- Tax collection systems
- Legal codes and standardization
- Religious legitimacy
- Integration of diverse populations

### 3.3 Empires Collapse (Later Period)
- Overextension
- Economic strain from military spending
- Internal conflicts
- External pressures

---

## The Gunpowder Empires

### Ottoman Empire (1299-1922)
- **Capital**: Constantinople (Istanbul) after 1453
- **Religion**: Sunni Islam
- **Government**: Sultan with absolute power; devshirme system
- **Key features**: 
  - Janissaries (elite slave soldiers)
  - Millet system (religious tolerance)
  - Control of Mediterranean and Middle East trade

### Safavid Empire (1501-1736)
- **Capital**: Isfahan
- **Religion**: Shia Islam (state religion)
- **Government**: Shah with absolute power
- **Key features**:
  - Shia identity vs. Sunni Ottomans
  - Persian cultural revival
  - Silk trade wealth

### Mughal Empire (1526-1857)
- **Capital**: Delhi, then Agra
- **Religion**: Islam ruling Hindu majority
- **Government**: Emperor with zamindars (regional lords)
- **Key features**:
  - Religious syncretism under Akbar
  - Magnificent architecture (Taj Mahal)
  - Cotton textile production

---

## Other Major Empires

### Ming Dynasty (1368-1644)
- Restored Han Chinese rule after Mongols
- Civil service exams strengthened
- Great Wall rebuilt
- Zheng He's voyages, then maritime withdrawal
- Fell to Manchu invasion → Qing Dynasty

### Qing Dynasty (1644-1912)
- Manchu conquest of China
- Retained Chinese bureaucracy
- Expanded into Central Asia, Tibet, Taiwan
- Queue hairstyle required

### Russian Empire
- Expansion across Siberia to Pacific
- Ivan IV (Ivan the Terrible) centralized power
- Serfdom intensified
- Peter the Great's Westernization (1682-1725)

### Tokugawa Japan (1603-1868)
- Shogun unified Japan after civil war
- Strict social hierarchy (samurai, farmers, artisans, merchants)
- Sakoku policy: Isolation from West
- Peace and stability for 250 years

### Songhai Empire (1464-1591)
- Largest empire in African history
- Controlled trans-Saharan trade
- Islamic learning center at Timbuktu
- Defeated by Moroccan invasion (1591)

## Methods of Maintaining Power

| Method | Examples |
|--------|----------|
| **Military** | Janissaries (Ottoman), Banner system (Qing), Samurai (Japan) |
| **Bureaucracy** | Civil service exams (China), devshirme (Ottoman) |
| **Religion** | Divine right, religious legitimacy, official faiths |
| **Economy** | Tax farming, land grants, trade monopolies |
| **Integration** | Millet system (Ottoman), marriage alliances |
| **Coercion** | Public executions, deportations, surveillance |

## Cause and Effect Relationships

### Rise of Gunpowder Empires
**Causes:**
- Gunpowder technology spread from China
- Decline of Mongol successor states
- Ambitious leaders with military innovation

**Effects:**
- Larger, more centralized empires
- Professional armies replaced cavalry-based warfare
- Increased tax revenue for military
- Displacement of older empires

### Ottoman Millet System
**Causes:**
- Religious diversity in conquered territories
- Need for social stability
- Islamic legal traditions for non-Muslims

**Effects:**
- Religious minorities maintained autonomy
- Reduced rebellion
- Preserved distinct communities
- Long-term: Nationalism eventually fragments empire

### Mughal Decline
**Causes:**
- Aurangzeb's religious intolerance
- Military overextension
- Economic strain
- Rise of regional powers (Marathas)

**Effects:**
- Fragmentation of empire
- British East India Company gains power
- Hindu-Muslim tensions increase

## Comparisons

### The Three "Gunpowder Empires"
| Feature | Ottoman | Safavid | Mughal |
|---------|---------|---------|--------|
| **Religion** | Sunni Islam | Shia Islam | Islam (Hindu majority) |
| **Elite military** | Janissaries | Qizilbash | Mansabdari system |
| **Religious policy** | Millet system | Shia conversion | Tolerance (Akbar) |
| **Economic base** | Trade, tribute | Silk trade | Agriculture, textiles |
| **Decline** | 1700s-1900s | 1722 (Afghan invasion) | 1700s (British takeover) |

### China: Ming vs. Qing
| Feature | Ming | Qing |
|---------|------|------|
| **Rulers** | Han Chinese | Manchu |
| **Expansion** | Limited | Tibet, Central Asia, Taiwan |
| **Foreign policy** | Isolation after Zheng He | Tributary system |
| **Civil service** | Confucian exams | Kept Ming system |
